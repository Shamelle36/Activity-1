What is Republic Act 10630?


An Act Strengthening the Juvenile Justice System in the Philippines, Amending for the Purpose Republic Act No. 9344, Otherwise Known as the "Juvenile Justice and Welfare Act of 2006" (or simply RA 10630) is a law passed in the Philippines in 2013 that aims to protect the rights of children in conflict with the law (CICLs) and promote their rehabilitation and reintegration into society.

Here are some of the features of Republic Act 10630:


Raising the Minimum Age of Criminal Responsibility:

  • Originally set at 9 years old under RA 9344, RA 10630 raised the minimum age of criminal responsibility (MCR) to 15 years old.
  • This means children below 15 are exempt from criminal liability and cannot be prosecuted in court.

    • Focus on Rehabilitation and Diversion:

  • RA 10630 emphasizes restorative justice and rehabilitation for children in conflict with the law (CICLs).
  • It expands diversion programs aimed at keeping CICLs out of the formal justice system and preventing criminalization.
  • These programs may include counseling, community service, and education initiatives.

    • Improved Detention Conditions:

  • The Act establishes stricter guidelines for the detainment of CICLs, ensuring separation from adult offenders and prioritizing their welfare.
  • It calls for child-friendly facilities with trained personnel and access to education, healthcare, and recreational activities.

    • Enhanced Rights and Due Process:

  • RA 10630 strengthens safeguards for the rights of CICLs during judicial proceedings.
  • This includes access to legal counsel, interpretation services, and protection from self-incrimination.

    • Community Involvement:

  • The Act encourages community involvement in supporting CICLs and their families.
  • This includes establishing Bahay Pag-asa facilities for short-term residential care and promoting reintegration programs.

    • Criticisms and Ongoing Debates:

  • While RA 10630 received praise for raising the MCR and prioritizing rehabilitation, some argue it doesn't go far enough.
  • Concerns remain about overloaded diversion programs, the effectiveness of rehabilitation efforts, and potential loopholes in the raised MCR.
